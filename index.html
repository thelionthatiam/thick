    I came home to find the tree gone.


    I had seen photographs of a tree limb larger than my body in diameter pressed close—almost crushing, the south end of my grandmother’s home—the branches pushing at and filling the glass of the bedroom, obscuring the sun.


    I had been told that it was cut, the wood moved by logging truck on the afternoon ferry across Elliott Bay to Bainbridge Island to stand in amongst large piles of other felled giants on the cleared chip laden site of a former saw mill. I came back to find it gone, save for a 4-foot stump—a stub pushing up from the ground so that rather than holding the yard in embrace from above, it looked to be more a navel, protruding up from a grass-moss belly of yard.


    Something had been cut. A cord (many cords, of wood, in fact), though it hadn’t been felled for heat.


    It had been felled for fear of falling. For fear that the disease moving slowly in it would take it anyway. It was felled to finish what was already in motion.


    So I climbed the stump and stood where old wood had once stood, supported, and departed, and counted the rings.


    One hundred and twenty-seven rings remembers one hundred and twenty-seven years.


    One hundred and twenty-seven years remembers the arrival of my Great Great Grandfather into the same harbour out of which the felled tree had just left.


    It remembers, that same year, the burning of a nascent, wooden city, and the making of state from territory.


    The seventh ring remembers gold pieces in Yukon rivers and men packing up lives to march on foot through thousands of miles of forest to search and die in the cold. To strike with pick and axe and hope it might make them rich.


    Men who could not—for their culture and circumstances and language and stories and experience—see the trees around them, the birch, the medicinal plants, the means for existing in reciprocity and listening relationship with the land.


    And within its first ring, within each cell of wood and bark and root, is the memory of the tree before it.


    And because it is an English Elm, a tree not born of seed, but cloned from the root shoots of clones before it, we may say that it is also Columella’s Elm, a Roman Elm. An Elm of Empires.


    An Elm of three empires spanning two continents and two-thousand years.
    An Elm cloned and grown to train the vines of vineyards and provide leaf hay.
    An Elm harvested for the heavy straight keels of the Queen’s Navy.
    An Elm cloned and grown along Highland hedgerows to ensure the division of land once held in common.
    An Elm cloned and grown and planted for shade along the avenues of titled nobility.
    An Elm lining the winding boulevards of Olmsted Brother’s many urban visions of ordered nature.


    An Elm raised in Columella’s garden, and now tracing its way to my grandmother’s garden.


    I stood, my body contained within one-hundred and twenty-seven rings, and slipped my fingernails into the thin space between dark wet wood and thick, grey cork bark, into the still slick space of xylem—the last living ring of a felled and dying tree.


    Xylem was where the fungus had sought life. A fungus brought by beetle from the East. The Scolytus Scolytus--the ancient yet fervent acolyte of Ulmus Procera.


    Xylem which draws up moisture and nutrients from the roots—helping to form new wood as it goes.


    Choking in the xylem.


    Echoing a selfsame death. A death rippling back across the continent. Back to the titled lands and hedgerows. Back to the vineyards of France and Spain. Back to Columella; who’s tree is gone. Gone in the East, and now gone in the west.


    Echoing the two eras of selfsame death of the Elms of Europe 1,000 and 4,000 years before Columella.


    Other moments when the balance fell away, and the beetle pushed in and the tree choked in the xylem.


    So now a navel pushing up from a belly yard.
